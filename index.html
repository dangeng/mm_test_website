TL;DR

We present a method to magnify subtle motions that can be trained without ground truth magnified videos. Our method also allows users to "target" the magnification, only magnifying specific regions or objects. Furthermore, being unsupervised our method also supports test-time adaptation for improved performance.

Approach Overview

Given a reference frame \(I_0\), a frame to be magnified \(I_t\), a differentiable optical flow estimator \(\mathcal{F}(\cdot, \cdot)\), and the predicted magnified frame \(\tilde{I}_t\), we optimize a motion magnification loss \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{mag}}\) which encourages correctly magnified motion as well as a visual consistency loss \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{color}}\) which regularizes the magnification loss and encourages correctly colored predictions.

Abstract

Magnifying motion in videos allows us to reveal subtle details that may be invisible to the naked eye. However, this task presents considerable challenges, due to the absence of "ground truth" magnified videos. In this paper, we revisit the classic Lagrangian approach in motion magnification and integrate it with modern self-supervised learning techniques.

Given an input sequence and a magnification factor, we generate another sequence whose optical flow has been amplified by a desired amount. To train our model, we create a loss function that applies optical flow to a generated video and measures how far its generated flow deviates from the given magnification factor. Thus, training the model involves differentiating through a pretrained optical flow network.

A key advantage of our approach is that it can be trained solely from unlabeled video data. As our method does not require any ground truth labels, we can also perform test-time adaptation, further improving our generalization to out-of-domain and complex videos. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach through evaluations of both visual quality and quantitative metrics on a diverse range of real-world videos and synthetic data.

Our model can also magnify different areas of a video different amounts. We achieve this by designing our architecture to accept a spatial map of magnification factors. This "targeting" ability works even when our method is trained only on spatial maps of constant magnification factors. This ability is useful when we want to focus on a specific object, as in the cat sequence, or when we want to ignore an object that may be challenging to magnify, such as the quickly moving arm in the bookshelf sequence.
